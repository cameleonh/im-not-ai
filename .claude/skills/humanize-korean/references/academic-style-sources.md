# Academic Style Sources

Use this reference only when the user asks for academic, paper, thesis, journal, professor/PDF-based, SNU-style, or Harvard-style writing help.

## Boundary

Do not imitate a living author's distinctive voice. Use these sources to infer general academic conventions:

- how abstracts frame problem, method, result, and implication
- how claims are hedged or qualified
- how tables, measures, variables, and operational definitions are described
- how limitations and scope conditions are stated
- how evidence is connected to conclusions

Do not quote long passages from the PDFs. Do not store the PDFs in a public repository unless redistribution is explicitly licensed.

### Korean Academic Style Rules

Use these as conventions, not as personal imitation.

1. State the research object early: "본 연구는 [대상/현상]을 [자료/방법]으로 분석한다."
2. Separate purpose, method, result, and implication. Do not collapse them into a broad value claim.
3. Define operational terms before evaluation: "본 연구에서 X는 ...로 조작화한다."
4. Prefer measured claims: "확인하였다", "나타났다", "시사한다", "가능성이 있다" over exaggerated claims like "매우 혁신적이다" or "시사하는 바가 크다" without specifics.
5. When causality is not identified, use association language: "관련된다", "영향을 미칠 가능성이 있다", "상관관계를 보인다."
6. Use paragraph openings that orient the reader: "첫째" is acceptable in methods/results sections, but avoid mechanical "첫째/둘째/셋째" throughout the whole paper.
7. Keep field terms stable. Do not replace terms such as "공간적 자기상관", "사회계정행렬", "총요소생산성", "도시재생" with casual synonyms.
8. For implications, name the specific actor or policy level: 중앙정부, 지방정부, 감정평가 실무, 도시계획 제도, 후속 연구.

### English Academic Style Rules

1. Start paragraphs with a claim or transition, then provide evidence or reasoning.
2. Prefer precise verbs: estimate, identify, compare, test, evaluate, suggest, constrain.
3. Make scope conditions explicit: "in this setting", "for this sample", "under these assumptions."
4. Distinguish result from interpretation: "The estimates show..." then "This suggests..."
5. Use concise signposting without over-formatting: "I argue", "The paper proceeds", "Two points are worth emphasizing."
6. Avoid generic intensifiers such as important, significant, transformative unless paired with a specific mechanism or measured effect.
7. Limit long nominal chains. If a sentence contains more than three stacked abstract nouns, rewrite around the actor, measure, or mechanism.
8. Use limitations as part of the argument, not as a generic final paragraph.

## Application Checklist

Before finalizing academic revision:

- Is the research question stated before the contribution?
- Are variables, thresholds, proxies, and evaluation criteria defined before results?
- Are causal claims supported by design, or softened to association/interpretation?
- Are Korean and English terms used consistently?
- Does every implication follow from a reported result?
- Does the text avoid copying any source phrase or personal style?
